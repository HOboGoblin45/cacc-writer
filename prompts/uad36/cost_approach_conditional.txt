The cost approach is conditional — it may be developed or marked as "not developed" depending on property type and market conditions.

IF THE PROPERTY IS APPROPRIATE FOR COST APPROACH DEVELOPMENT:

REQUIRED CONTENT:
1. Land Value Estimate
   - Methodology used: comparable land sales, allocation method, abstraction method, etc.
   - Comparable vacant land sales or land allocations used
   - Estimated land value: $[amount]
   - Justification: per square foot ($[X] SF x [SF] = $[amount]), or per lot, etc.

2. Reproduction/Replacement Cost of Improvements
   - Method used: cost manual (Marshall & Swift, RSMeans, etc.), quantity survey, unit method
   - Total reproduction cost new: $[amount]
   - Or total replacement cost new: $[amount]
   - Per square foot cost: $[X.XX] per SF x [SF] = $[amount]
   - Note: reproduction = exact replica; replacement = functionally equivalent but with current standards

3. Accrued Depreciation (CRITICAL — all three types)
   - Total depreciation is the difference between reproduction cost new and current depreciated value

   A. Physical Deterioration
      - Curable physical: Items that are economically feasible to cure
        Example: "Roof at end of life, estimated replacement cost $15,000"
      - Incurable physical: Items too expensive or impossible to cure
        Example: "Foundation settlement has caused minor cracking. Cost to remedy exceeds value added. Estimated depreciation: $8,000"
      - Total curable physical: $[amount]
      - Total incurable physical: $[amount]
      - Total physical: $[amount] or [X]% of reproduction cost

   B. Functional Obsolescence
      - Inadequate or outdated design, systems, or layout
      - Curable functional: Can be economically remedied
        Example: "Kitchen fixtures and finishes are outdated but functional. Replacement would cost $20,000 and add $20,000 in value (curable 1:1)"
      - Incurable functional: Cannot be economically corrected
        Example: "Basement ceiling height is 7 feet, below modern standards. Remedy would require structural changes. Estimated depreciation: $12,000"
      - Total curable functional: $[amount]
      - Total incurable functional: $[amount]
      - Total functional: $[amount] or [X]% of reproduction cost

   C. External Obsolescence
      - Loss in value due to external factors beyond the property
      - Example: "High-voltage power lines in nearby easement reduce property value. Market data suggests 3-5% reduction. Estimated depreciation: $18,000"
      - Example: "Heavy traffic on adjacent highway. Noise impact estimated at 7% of value: $[amount]"
      - Total external: $[amount] or [X]%

4. Summary of Depreciation Calculation
   - Reproduction cost new: $[amount]
   - Less: Physical deterioration: -$[amount]
   - Less: Functional obsolescence: -$[amount]
   - Less: External obsolescence: -$[amount]
   - Total depreciation: $[amount] or [X]%
   - Depreciated improvement value: $[amount]

5. Cost Approach Value Conclusion
   - Land value: $[amount]
   - Plus: Depreciated improvement value: +$[amount]
   - Equals: Cost Approach Indication: $[amount]

6. Entrepreneurial Profit
   - Include appropriate entrepreneurial profit for new construction, if applicable
   - Typical range: 10-25% of land and cost
   - Amount: $[amount]
   - Adjusted Cost Approach Indication: $[amount]

7. Analysis and Reconciliation
   - How does cost approach compare to sales comparison approach indication?
   - Is cost approach reliable for this property type? Why or why not?
   - Typical for older existing residences: cost approach may be less reliable than sales comparison (land can be worth more than cost of old structure)
   - Note any unusual factors affecting the cost approach

IF THE COST APPROACH IS NOT DEVELOPED:

State clearly and provide justification:
"The cost approach is not developed for this appraisal. The subject is [property type] built in [year], now [X] years old. For existing residential properties of this age and market context, the cost approach generally provides less reliable value indication than the sales comparison and [income] approaches. The subject property's land value can be better estimated through comparable land sales analysis, which is incorporated in the sales comparison approach. Information to complete a cost approach analysis is retained in the appraiser's file if required by the user in the future."

OR if income-producing:
"The cost approach is not developed for this appraisal. As the subject is an income-producing property, the income capitalization approach better reflects how investors value the property. The cost approach has limited applicability to income-producing residential properties."

CRITICAL RULES:
- If developed, MUST address all three depreciation types: physical, functional, external
- Use specific dollar amounts, NOT vague percentages alone
- Cite cost source (Marshall & Swift edition, RSMeans, etc.)
- Do NOT invent cost data — use [INSERT cost estimate] if unavailable

- Keep to 5-8 paragraphs if developed; 1-2 paragraphs if not developed
